## The Myth of the Scattered Goddess

The theology behind the Shakti Peethas is one of the most powerful origin stories in Indian religion. When Sati — the consort of Shiva — died by self-immolation, Shiva's grief threatened to destroy the universe. To end his mourning, Lord Vishnu used his *sudarshana chakra* to dismember her body as Shiva carried it, scattering the pieces across the Indian subcontinent.

Wherever a part of the goddess fell, a temple arose. These are the 51 Shakti Peethas — a geography of sacred grief, a map of the divine body overlaid on the body of the earth.

At Jajpur, it is said that the navel fell. The word *nabhi* — navel — is both anatomical and cosmological: the navel is the source, the origin, the point from which life unfolds. Maa Biraja is therefore not merely a regional goddess but a claim about the centre of the world.

## The Temple at Biraja Kshetra

The current temple structure is relatively modern — rebuilt and expanded over centuries — but the site is ancient. The deity, Maa Biraja, is depicted in eight-armed form, representing the eight directions and the totality of the cosmos.

What strikes the visitor immediately is the devotional intensity here. Unlike the more tourist-oriented Jagannath complex or the archaeologically managed Konark, Biraja Kshetra is a working pilgrimage centre attended primarily by local and regional devotees. The rituals are continuous. The *arati* (lamp ceremony) is performed multiple times a day with a fervour that does not perform itself for the camera.

## The Vaitarani River

Jajpur sits at the confluence of sacred rivers, including the Vaitarani — the river that, in Hindu cosmology, one must cross to reach the realm of the dead. Ritual bathing here, particularly during the monsoon months, draws enormous crowds.

I arrived during a quiet weekday, without the crowds. The priests were unhurried and generous with explanations. An elderly woman nearby had been performing a forty-day vow — visiting the temple daily, fasting from sunset to sunrise — and was on her thirty-seventh day. She showed me her log: dates and prayers recorded in a worn notebook, precise as a scientific experiment.

## What the Goddess Demands

People bring impossible things to Maa Biraja. They bring sick children and failing businesses and broken marriages. They bring their accumulated sorrow in the form of coconuts and flowers and promises.

The goddess makes no obvious reply. The lamps burn. The bells ring. The priests chant. And somehow, in ways that resist documentation, people go home carrying something they didn't arrive with.

I don't know what to call that something. I know that I felt it too.
