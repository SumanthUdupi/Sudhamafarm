## The Grand Road

They call it *Bada Danda* — the Grand Road. It stretches from the eastern gate of the Jagannath Temple all the way to the Gundicha Temple, a distance of three kilometres that, on the day of Rath Yatra, becomes the most densely populated strip of earth on the planet.

I arrived in Puri in the early morning hours, before the pilgrimage crowds woke. The town smelled of salt air and burning ghee. The lanes leading to the Singhadwara — the Lion Gate — were already filling with flower sellers and prasad shops, their pyramids of laddus wrapped in red cloth catching the first light.

## The Four Gates

The Jagannath Temple complex is entered through four gates, each facing a cardinal direction, each governed by a different deity. The Singhadwara on the east is the main entrance for devotees. Two stone lions flank it, and the flagpole above — the Nilachakra — is visible for miles, even from the sea.

Non-Hindus are not permitted inside. This is not cruelty but theology: the outer world is considered impure in a way that is incompatible with the sanctum. A watchtower on the perimeter allows those of other faiths to glimpse the *sudarshana chakra* atop the spire.

## Maha Prasad: Food of the Gods

Within the temple complex sits what may be the largest active kitchen in the world. Fifty-six varieties of food are cooked daily in earthen pots, stacked seven high over wood fires, and offered first to Lord Jagannath before being distributed as *Maha Prasad*.

The Ananda Bazaar — the Market of Bliss — is where the prasad is sold. The belief is profound and practical: whatever is prepared here is cooked by the goddess Mahalakshmi herself. No distinction of caste or creed applies to those who receive it. The rice and dal that I ate, wrapped in a banana leaf, were the best I have ever tasted — not because of spice or refinement, but because of what they carried.

## The Rath Yatra

Once a year, the three deities — Jagannath, his brother Balabhadra, and his sister Subhadra — are brought out of the temple and placed in enormous wooden chariots. Thousands of devotees pull these chariots with ropes along the Bada Danda. It is said that merely witnessing this is sufficient for liberation.

The chariots are built fresh each year from specific trees — a tradition that ensures the entire structure, down to its last wooden pin, carries the energy of renewal. Nothing is recycled from the previous year's ratha.

## The Sea at Evening

After the temple, I walked to the beach. The Bay of Bengal was rough and grey, the sand black in the fading light. Fishermen were pulling nets. Children flew kites. Somewhere behind me, a thousand lamps were being lit in the name of a god who wears a smile that no human sculptor has ever fully explained.

I sat there a long time. The wind from the sea smelled of distance.
