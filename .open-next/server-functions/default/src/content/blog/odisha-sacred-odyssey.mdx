## The Golden Triangle

Odisha's three sacred cities — Bhubaneswar, Konark, and Puri — form a triangle across the coastal plain that archaeologists call a pilgrimage circuit and that travellers call an education in what human beings are capable of building when they believe in something with their entire civilization.

I drove the triangle in three days. That is not enough time. It is, however, enough time to understand why you need to come back.

## Day One: The Temple City

Bhubaneswar earned its title "the temple city of India" the old-fashioned way — by building, at various points between the 7th and 12th centuries, an estimated seven thousand temples. About five hundred survive in varying states of preservation.

The Lingaraj Temple is the crown. But the real discovery, for me, was the cluster of smaller temples in the Old Town — the Parashurameshwar, the Mukteshvara (with its extraordinary *torana*, a carved stone gateway that represents the high-water mark of Kalinga decorative art), and the Rajarani, named not for royalty but for the particular red-and-gold sandstone — *raja rani* stone — from which it is built.

## Day Two: The Wheel

Konark in the early morning is cold and quiet. I arrived before the gates opened and walked around the outer perimeter of the *jagamohana* while the light was still horizontal and gold. The stone wheels cast their shadows across the sand. A peacock called from somewhere inside the protected enclosure. A man was performing yoga on the lawn, facing the east.

The Archaeological Survey of India has done good work here in recent decades — stabilizing the structure, clearing encroachments, lighting the site carefully at night. The sound-and-light show in the evening is theatrical but surprisingly moving.

## Day Three: Puri and the Sea

The road from Konark to Puri runs along the coast. For stretches of it, you can see both the temple spires and the ocean simultaneously. This corridor — sacred architecture and primordial water — is one of the great visual experiences available in India.

Puri itself is two cities layered on top of each other. The pilgrimage city is ancient, dense, organized around the Jagannath Temple like a wheel around its axle. The beach town is modern, loud, and entirely separate in spirit. The skill is to inhabit both simultaneously — to eat *mahaprasad* at noon and watch the sunset over the Bay of Bengal in the evening.

## What Remains

I drove back to Bhubaneswar on the last day with the particular feeling that travel through places of genuine spiritual power tends to produce: not peace exactly, not excitement, but a kind of recalibration. The ordinary world had been briefly shown its own ordinariness. The extraordinary had been briefly shown to be possible.

The temples of Odisha do not ask for belief. They ask only for attention. That is a much harder thing, and a much more rewarding one.
